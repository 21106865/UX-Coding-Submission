The Environmental Impact of Cryptocurrency

Cryptocurrency: the modern-day coal?

‘Mining Crypto’ has become a household term after the surge of popularity into cryptocurrencies, with social media giants such as Elon Musk fuelling the frenzy into the industry.

Time magazine reports that the cryptocurrency industry is now worth more than $3 trillion, as investors are drawn into the prospects of making a lot of money by taking advantage of this cultural trend to move away from the traditional banking system.

How does someone acquire cryptocurrency?

Bitcoin is rewarded to those who complete a block of verified transactions, that are then added to a blockchain. In other words, you must complete an extremely complex computational maths problem, and the computer that solves the problem first receives the next block of bitcoin. This process is known as mining, and like its name counterpart using huge machinery to extract minerals from the earth, the sophisticated hardware required to complete the problems means there’s a huge capital requirement to start the crypto mining process.

Cryptocurrency is painstaking, costly, and only sporadically rewarding.
Euny Hony, Investopedia (2022)

The environmental implications

Cryptocurrency mining is highly energy-intensive due to the operations cost of the hardware needed to run the algorithms. Fossil fuels are being used as the primary source of energy to mine cryptocurrency, Digiconomist estimates that Bitcoin is responsible for 144 million tons of carbon dioxide per year, equivalent to the amount the Czech Republic produces.
